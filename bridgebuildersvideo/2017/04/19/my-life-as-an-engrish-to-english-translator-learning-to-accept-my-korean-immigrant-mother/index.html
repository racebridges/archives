MY LIFE AS AN “ENGRISH TO ENGLISH” TRANSLATOR: LEARNING TO ACCEPT MY KOREAN IMMIGRANT MOTHER

Posted on 04/19/2017 by bridgebuildersvideo

by Storyteller Elizabeth Gomez
Elizabeth Gomez is a stand-up comedian and storyteller based in Chicago, IL. Elizabeth is the daughter of a Korean mother and a military man father who is Puerto Rican American. They met in Korea. Her mother spent most of her years traveling the world and very little of it in the U.S. by the time the father left them. When the dad was on military mission Elizabeth’s mother had to figure out how to get along without him. Gradually Elizabeth became the “translator” and “interpreter” for her Korean mother who never fully learned English or “fitted” into the American culture.

This is the story of Elizabeth’s forced child to adult mission of being an interpreter and translator for her Korean mother. She lost school friends because of her mom’s customs and broken English. She took a stand when the young Elizabeth discovers her dad’s girlfriend.  The story spotlights the “dance” between daughter and mother as both cross cultures and struggle to be understood and to belong.  ~10 minutes.

Comments & Reflections

“… I never understood why my mother named me Elizabeth since she never has been able to pronounce it. Eventually she started calling me Lisa, which she also could not pronounce…. School conferences were the worst. Watching my mother talk to my well-educated and articulate teachers was petrifying and humiliating. They didn’t understand her and we all knew it. I’d watch my teachers stare at my mother with plastered smiles, nodding their heads as she gesticulated what she was trying to say, which ultimately was, “Risa, she so crazy.”

“ …. When my friends would come over, I would shrink under the table as my mother would pinch them and exclaim, “Why you so fat?!” …. So, I stopped inviting kids over and accepted life as the town recluse with the rude mother….”

(From the video story by Elizabeth Gomez)

Pondering the Story

This story is about the classic struggle to adapt to the language and the cultures of the USA. It is also echoes the stories of many immigrant families and the younger generations that are born or come of age in the USA. Between the old and the new. Between the language of the parent and the language and lifestyles of the son or daughter.

The following questions are used with this RaceBridges video-story for broad online audiences. Can you translate in your thinking and pondering of how this mother-daughter experience relates to you and your history? To your ministry?

✿ How many of you are recent immigrants or have immigrant parents?

✿ What are the daily struggles you have or that you see your parents and other family members going through because of intercultural gaps and divides?

✿ If you have immigrant parents, are there times you are embarrassed by them? Can you share examples and reflect on from where the embarrassment comes from?

✿ What do people who have been here longer need to understand and how can they be a support to new immigrants?
