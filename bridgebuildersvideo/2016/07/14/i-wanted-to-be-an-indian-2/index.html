I WANTED TO BE AN INDIAN

Posted on 07/14/2016 by bridgebuildersvideo

By Storyteller Jo Radner
Stories about our ancestors can help us understand who we are. Sometimes in those
family and community histories we discover evil and darkness as well as pride and dignity. Jo Radner encounters troubling revelations about her forebears and their Indian neighbors in colonial New England.  Jo asks what it means to tell – and live with – her whole, complex history. Length – 8 minutes.

I WANTED TO BE AN INDIAN

Storyteller Jo Radner
(Length : 8 minutes )

EXPLORING THE STORY

Summary

Storyteller Jo Radner tells the story of her family heritage in the the towns of New England in colonial times. Intertwined with her family history is the decimation of native American peoples and in particular the Abenaki Indians of New England and Canada.

Radner recalls the wish of her childhood to be an Indian.  Later she learns of her family history and the complex true story of an Indian slaughter carried out by one of her family ancestors. Emerging from this family history is also the haunting story of the same person living with the Abenaki Indian tribe for eight years.   This story explores how Jo Radner seeks to balance the good and the bad of her history and how she seeks to deal with that narrative today.

“However, consciously or unconsciously, tensions and conflicts inevitably arise among confreres due to generational gaps, racism, ethnocentrism, personality differences and stereotyping. Confreres may be tempted to avoid the challenge of enriching intercultural experiences… either by imposing a uniform lifestyle, or by living in mutual  indifference…”  Documents of the 17th General Chapter SVD 2012

“ … Do I still want to be an Indian ?  No.  I want to learn to live well with my whole history.

To recognize the monstrosities and the kindnesses that lie behind me.

To make family of all kinds. To melt my own icy heart. “

 (From the video story by Jo Radner)

Ponderings

From Jo Radner’s Story :

“ …. My grandmother told me that my English ancestors had founded several towns in Massachusetts, New Hampshire and Maine. I was proud.  We’ve been here since 1635 ! But when I studied history I realized what it meant to found all these towns .   My descendants had been among the first to take the Indians’ lands, to cut down the forests, to fence the fields…to feel entitled to destroy the way of life of the native peoples.  Then when I studied my own family history later on I discovered more things I didn’t want to know…”

When do “heroic” stories become the horror stories ? 

“…It’s all in who gets to tell the stories … and what stories they choose to tell….”

People say that in history, the winners get to tell the stories. How do we look beyond the winners’ points of view to understand the past?

“…I wish my family had been able to live kindly and peacefully. I wish history had taken a different turn. John Farrington was an Indian fighter all his life. But in some sense he was still an Abenaki Indian….”

How do we deal with dark stories that emerge from our family tree and family histories ?

“…. An Abenaki legend is told about a cannibal monster with an icy heart who comes to devour a small family.  But the mother of the family welcomes him as if he’s her father. She washes him and dresses him. She and her husband tell him family stories. They treat him like a beloved relative.  And the monster sits surly for 3 days. And then he drinks a kettle of boiling grease.   It melts his icy heart. It purges all the evil that he has done. And after that he lives with the family and takes care of them…”

Is the Abenaki story a viable model for bridging cultures? In practical terms, how might we treat “the other” as family?
