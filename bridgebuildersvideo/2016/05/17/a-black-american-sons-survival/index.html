by Storyteller Sheila Arnold
A frantic call from Sheila Arnold’s son during his freshmen year in college turns into a moment to remember all that she had to teach him about growing up black, and, in turn, all he had also learned about crossing bridges in spite of people’s perceptions.

EXPLORING THE STORY

Summary

African American Storyteller Sheila Arnold tells of the urgent phone call from her son who had just begun his college years. Sheila’s son pleads with her that he wants to come home. He pleads, “I want to come home. People here are racist …”

Arnold’s college aged son, Chris, makes good friends with an Irish American student. But as they hang out together he begins to notice the subtle everyday racist exclusion he meets – small and not so small.

Sheila reflects about the life lessons that she taught her son as he grew up and encountered the world as a young Black male.

Arnold’s story ends with a later phone call from her son Chris. The challenges remained for him but he had found a strength within as he sought his identity.

 


Comments & Reflections

“However, consciously or unconsciously, tensions and conflicts inevitably arise among confreres due to generational gaps, racism, ethnocentrism, personality differences and stereotyping. Confreres may be tempted to avoid the challenge of enriching intercultural experiences… either by imposing a uniform lifestyle, or by living in mutual indifference…”
Documents of the 17th General Chapter SVD 2012

“…I had talked to him (my college aged African American son) about survival and staying
alive as much as I talked to him about English. Math, History, and Biology. This was
important for his life. But I still couldn’t prepare him for what would happen in his heart
when the racism was so blatant. I couldn’t help him when that racism hit him full frontal…”

(From the video-story by Sheila Arnold)

Ponderings

  •  What have been our responses to outright racist actions ?
  •  How have our responses been to press and news reports of outright racist actions?
  •  How have our responses been to the racist joke or comment at the meal table ?
  •  Sheila Arnold’s worries and advice to her college aged son are repeated still among parents of color as they anguish about the safety of their sons on the streets of America’s cities as the long hot summer begins.
  • Part of the intercultural journey is standing in the shoes of the “other”.

Flowing on from this video story is a continuing stark reality.  It is found in the words of Otis Moss, the Senior Pastor at Chicago’s south side Trinity United Church of Christ earlier this year. It was his plea to the church community to help Black boys survive. It was entitled Get Home Safely: 10 Rules of Survival If stopped by the Police.  This urgent message by the church was in the face of mounting police violence against young black men.
