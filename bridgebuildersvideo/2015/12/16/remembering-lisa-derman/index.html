REMEMBERING LISA DERMAN

Posted on 12/16/2015 by bridgebuildersvideo

by Storyteller Jim May

Theme :  All people will face a time when they must decide whether
to stand up for what is right.

Jim May of Spring Grove, IL recalls the hot day when Holocaust Survivor Lisa Derman told her last story at the Illinois Storytelling Festival. The searing account recalled her escape from the Nazis in World War II Poland.  During that performance Lisa Derman died of a fatal heart attack while telling the story she had shared thousands of times.

EXPLORING THE STORY

Summary

In “Remembering Lisa Derman,” storyteller Jim May tells the history of developing the Illinois Storytelling Festival, which included the stories of average people—“civilian storytellers” as he calls them. In 2002 the festival invited Holocaust survivors and those who had helped liberate the World War II concentration camps in Europe to come tell their stories.

Among the storytellers was Lisa Derman, a Holocaust survivor who told the story of escaping the slaughter of her fellow villagers and family in Poland.  She was protected by a Christian woman who hid Derman and her sister in defiance of the German SS.

While telling this story at the storytelling event, Derman challenged her listeners, saying that they, too, would one day face a decision and, like the Christian woman who took her in, they would have to decide whether or not to do the right thing.

Then, while telling her story on the stage of the Illinois Storytelling Festival, Derman had a heart attack that killed her instantly. The storytelling festival has created a memorial for Derman, so that future generations can heed her challenge to stand up for what is right.

“However, consciously or unconsciously, tensions and conflicts inevitably arise among confreres due to generational gaps, racism, ethnocentrism, personality differences and stereotyping. Confreres may be tempted to avoid the challenge of enriching intercultural experiences… either by imposing a uniform lifestyle, or by living in mutual indifference…”

“There will be a time when all of you will have to stand up and do what is right.
The call will come. And you must care and stand up and do what’s right.
I may not be here much longer,” she said, “but my story must go on.”

Comments & Reflections

    • May believes that anyone who has lived an interesting life has stories to tell; he calls these folks “civilian storytellers.” What story would you like to tell from your life? How might you live your life so that you have stories to tell later? Is this “story-creation” active in our ministries and in our communities ?
      Is it life-giving ?
    • Consider this well-known quotation:
      “      First they came for the Socialists, and I did not speak out—
      Because I was not a Socialist.
      Then they came for the Trade Unionists, and I did not speak out—
      Because I was not a Trade Unionist.
      Then they came for the Jews, and I did not speak out—
      Because I was not a Jew.
      Then they came for me—and there was no one left to speak for me.”
      Words of Pastor Martin Niemuller, fierce critic of  Nazis in World War II.
      . . . . .  Food for thought.
    • Why do you think the woman in Derman’s story decided to hide Derman and her sister from the German SS?  What do you think allowed some people to work against the Nazis, while others did nothing?
    • Derman said that all people will face a time when they have to stand up and do what is right. Have you ever had to stand up for what is right? What situations in the world today require us to stand up and do what is right? What can you do to develop the courage to do what is right?
      . . . . The Black Lives Matter Movement . . . Protest against Police Violence, Discrimination against Muslims ….Discrimination against Immigrants …. Hate crimes ….   There is much to protest still.  What stand will we take ?  What stand will I take ?
